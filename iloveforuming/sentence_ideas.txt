2. A wise man digs two wells before he feels thirsty.
3. Fate writes in ink, but the truly clever rewrite the page before it dries.
4. Every tombstone is a promise that someone once tried to live forever.
5. The river flows toward the sea, but the fish inside dream of swimming upstream.

32. A bridge built too beautifully becomes a monument, not a crossing.
33. The pearl grows inside pain, but the oyster still drowns when opened.

44. A storm respects no prayer, but it respects a strong roof.

46. A leaf falling in an empty forest still lands on someone’s forgotten grave.
47. The wise snake bites only when the boot lifts—never when it presses.
48. A promise carved in stone is just a boulder waiting for rain.
49. The wolf that guards the sheep is only waiting for a hungrier wolf.
50. Every road you do not take eventually grows over, but the ghosts remember.
51. A dagger in the front kills the body; a dagger in the back kills the trust of witnesses.
52. The monk who meditates on a mountaintop still shivers in winter.
53. A fire that warms your hands will burn your home if you turn your back.
54. The crow eats the farmer’s corn, then praises the scarecrow’s patience.
55. A kingdom built on gratitude collapses on the first dry year.
56. The well that gives water to everyone eventually goes dry from too many blessings.
57. A scar is a story that skin tells bone when no one else is listening.
58. The clever fisherman throws back the smallest fish to catch the school later.
59. A truth told at the wrong time is a sharper lie.
60. The mountain does not care which bird sings on its peak.
61. A debt paid late is a debt paid twice—once in coin, once in worry.
62. The fox who envies the eagle’s wings forgets the ground’s hidden seeds.
63. A candle in a cave shows you the walls, not the way out.
64. The arrow thanks the bow for the wound it causes.
65. A wise man plants a tree, but he also burns a neighbor’s forest just in case.
66. The sleeping viper is still a viper; the sleeping saint is just tired.
67. A gate left open is an invitation or a trap—the difference is the second gate.
68. The river does not fight the stone; it drowns the stone’s children instead.
69. A meal shared with an enemy is a meal poisoned twice—once for the body, once for memory.
70. The old wolf limps so the young wolves learn to limp later.
71. A name forgotten by history is a key that opens no lock but every door.
72. The bell that rings for the dead also wakes the thief.
73. A path marked by bones is still a path.
74. The seed that fears the dark never breaks the soil.
75. A bargain whispered in a cave echoes louder than a vow shouted on a mountain.

98. The hourglass runs faster when you stop watching it.
99. A wall built against enemies becomes a prison for friends if you build it too high.
100. The stars do not guide the lost—they simply burn until someone looks up.
